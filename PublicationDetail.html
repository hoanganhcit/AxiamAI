Why Online Hacking Will Continue to Increase if Anonymous Online Users Are Still Allowed - By John Kumar (January 8, 2025)

In today’s digital world, cybersecurity has become a constant battlefield. Organizations invest billions of dollars annually in advanced firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and multi-factor authentication (MFA). Yet, despite all these defenses, hackers continue to thrive. The reason is simple: the foundation of online identity remains broken. As long as cyberspace continues to rely on anonymous users identified only by usernames, passwords, and MFA codes, online hacking will not just persist—it will grow.

The internet was originally built on anonymity. Early platforms required only a username and password, enabling users to interact without exposing their true identities. While this approach encouraged open participation, it also created a system where individuals could easily impersonate, deceive, or exploit others without accountability.

This anonymous model has carried over into the modern internet. Today, almost every online account—from social media to financial services—still relies on the same fragile system of credentials. Even with MFA layered on top, the identity of the person behind the screen remains unverifiable. Hackers exploit this weakness daily, using stolen or faked credentials to masquerade as legitimate users.

Hackers excel because anonymity gives them room to operate. A stolen username and password can grant full access to sensitive systems. Even MFA, once hailed as a game changer, has proven vulnerable to phishing, SIM swapping, and real-time man-in-the-middle attacks. Attackers don’t need to physically break into systems; they just need to trick someone into giving them the keys.

Every successful hack—from account takeovers to ransomware attacks—traces back to the same fundamental issue: the system allows access based on what you know (a password) or what you have (a phone or token), not who you truly are. This lack of verified identity makes it almost impossible to fully stop cybercriminals.

The consequences of anonymous access are massive. Organizations lose billions in direct damages, recovery costs, and regulatory fines every year. More importantly, consumer trust erodes with every breach. People hesitate to adopt digital services when they feel their identities and assets are constantly at risk.

Cybercriminals, on the other hand, face little accountability. They can launch attacks from anywhere in the world under false identities, making prosecution difficult or impossible. As long as anonymity is protected online, hacking will remain a lucrative, low-risk crime. To reverse this trend, the digital world must move beyond usernames and passwords toward verified digital identity. Biometric authentication, such as facial recognition, ensures that the person accessing an account is the rightful owner—not just someone who stole credentials. Unlike passwords, biometrics cannot be forgotten, guessed, or phished.

By anchoring online access to true identity, we reduce the hacker’s advantage. Cybercriminals cannot hide behind anonymous usernames if every login is tied to a verifiable human being. While no system is perfect, shifting toward identity-based security significantly raises the barrier to entry for attackers.

Cyberattacks will continue to rise as long as anonymous online access remains the norm. The reliance on usernames, passwords, and MFA creates a weak foundation that hackers can and will exploit. If we truly want to make hacking harder to solve, the digital world must embrace verified identity. Without it, anonymity will remain the hacker’s greatest weapon, and online crime will only continue to escalate.
